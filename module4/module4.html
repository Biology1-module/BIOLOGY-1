Lesson 4: Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic

This supplementary module is designed to help you understand the difference between Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cell. In this lesson, you will explore how cell complexity contributes to diversity in organisms and in classifying life forms.

All living organisms are made of cells. Cells are classified as prokaryotic or eukaryotic. Understanding the differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells helps explain the diversity and classification of living things.

After going through this supplementary module, you are expected to:

  • Differentiate prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells using diagrams and descriptions;
  • Explain why cell complexity contributes to diversity in organisms; and
  • Connect cell structure to the classification of life forms.

Prokaryotic Cells

All prokaryotic cells are structurally simple. They can be divided into two main groups, called the domain Bacteria and domain Archaea. Bacteria are well known because they can cause serious diseases, such as tuberculosis, tetanus, and throat infections. Many species of bacteria are important to the environment because they contribute to ecological cycles and decompose remains of dead organisms. They can also be used for manufacturing some products such as medicines and food.

Unlike bacteria, Archaea, also known as archaebacteria, can live on extreme environments such as hypothermal vents and acidic or basic waters. Some of them can live in digestive tracts of animals and even on petroleum deposits underground. Archaea that can survive under extreme condition are called extremophiles.

Structures of a Prokaryotic Cell

  • Cell Membrane: A phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins which regulates the entrance and exit of substances into and out of the cytoplasm.
  • Mesosomes: Extension of cell membrane used for attachment of enzymes that carry on metabolic activities.
  • Cell wall: Maintains the shape of the cell. The cell wall of bacteria contains peptidoglycan which is a complex molecule containing a unique amino disaccharide and peptide fragments.
  • Cytoplasm: Semifluid solution composed of water and inorganic and organic molecules encased by the plasma membrane.
  • Glycocalyx: Layer of polysaccharides lying outside the cell wall. It functions as a receptor and protection to the cell.
  • Nucleoid: Region where DNA is located. The DNA is coiled up into a single chromosome. It also contains extra-chromosomal plasmids.
  • Sex Pilus: Rigid tubular structure used by bacteria to pass DNA from cell to cell.
  • Flagellum: Helps in the movement of the cell. It has a filament, a hook, and a basal body.
  • Fimbriae: Small, bristle-like fibers that sprout from the cell surface. They help in the attachment of bacteria to the surface.
  • Inclusion body: Serves as storage of various substances such as carbohydrates, glycogen, phosphate, and fats which can be used when needed.
  • Ribosome: Tiny particle where protein synthesis takes place.
  • Endospore: Helps the cell survive under harsh conditions.

Eukaryotic Cells

A eukaryotic cell is a type of cell found in plants, animals, protists, and fungi. Unlike a prokaryotic cell, it has a membrane-bounded nucleus which houses its DNA. Eukaryotic cells contain different organelles which perform different functions in the cell. Eukaryotes can be single-celled, colonial, or multicellular.

Structures of Eukaryotic Cells

  • Nucleus: Where the genetic material, deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA), is stored. It helps in the production of proteins and ribosomes.
  • Cytoplasm: Semifluid solution composed of water, inorganic and organic molecules that are encased by the cell membrane. It is where all organelles are located and most of cellular activities take place.
  • Mitochondrion: Responsible for production of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the energy source of cellular processes, and where cellular respiration happens.
  • Chloroplast: Can only be found in plants and algae. It is responsible for converting energy to chemical energy through the process of photosynthesis.
  • Golgi apparatus: Helps in the packaging and sorting of modified cell products of proteins and lipids.
  • Lysosome: Vesicle that digests intracellular molecules and even cell parts.
  • Vacuole: Responsible for engulfing materials through the process of endocytosis.
  • Endoplasmic reticulum (ER): Transports lipids, proteins, and other materials through the cell. The two types are smooth ER and rough ER.
  • Cilia and flagella: Hair-like projections that enables the cell to move. Cilia are much shorter than flagella, but they have similar construction.
  • Cell/ Plasma membrane: Phospholipid bilayer that regulates the entrance and exit of substances in the cell.
  • Ribosome: Particle that carry out protein synthesis.
  • Cytoskeleton: Responsible in maintaining the shape and framework of the cell. It is also responsible in assisting movement of cell organelles. It is composed of actin filaments, intermediate filaments, and microtubules.
