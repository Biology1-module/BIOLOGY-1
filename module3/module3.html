Lesson 3: Light vs Electron Microscope

This supplementary module is designed to help you understand the difference between a light and an electron microscope, one of the most important foundations in biology. In this lesson, you will explore the advantages and limitations of each microscope in observing cell.

A microscope is an essential scientific tool that allows us to see objects too small for the naked eye. The function is to magnify and reveal the fine details of cells and other tiny structures, helping us understand the microscopic world. In this lesson, you will learn about the structure and function of light and electron microscopes.

After going through this supplementary module, you are expected to:

  • Describe the structure and function of the light and electron microscopes.
  • Compare the magnification and resolution of light and electron microscopes.
  • Explain the advantages and limitations of each microscope in cell observation; and
  • Analyze information from secondary sources to support explanations about microscope use in cell study.

Light Microscope

A light microscope is an instrument used to magnify small objects such as cells and microorganisms that cannot be seen with the naked eye. It uses visible light and lenses to enlarge the image of a specimen, allowing scientists and students to observe fine details.

Parts and Function of a Light Microscope

  • Eyepiece (Ocular Lens): Located at the top of the microscope. Magnifies the image (usually 10×). This is where you look to see the specimen.
  • Nosepiece (Revolving Nosepiece): Holds the objective lenses. Rotates to change magnification.
  • Objective Lenses: Lenses of different magnifications attached to the nosepiece.
  • Stage: Flat platform where the slide is placed. Has clips to hold the slide in place.
  • Light Source / Mirror: Provides light that passes through the specimen. Essential for making the specimen visible.
  • Condenser: Focuses light onto the specimen. Improves image clarity.
  • Diaphragm: Adjusts the amount of light reaching the specimen.
  • Coarse Adjustment Knob: Moves the stage up and down quickly. Used for initial focusing.
  • Fine Adjustment Knob: Moves the stage slowly. Used for sharpening the image.
  • Arm: Supports the upper part of the microscope. Used to carry the microscope.
  • Base: Bottom part that supports the microscope. Provides stability.
  • Body Tube: Connects the eyepiece to the objective lenses.

Electron Microscope

An electron microscope is a powerful scientific instrument that uses a beam of electrons instead of light to magnify extremely small structures, allowing us to see details inside cells that are invisible under a light microscope.

Parts and Function of Electron Microscope

  • Electron Gun: Produces a beam of electrons using a heated filament (like tungsten). This replaces the light source in a light microscope. Function: Generates the electrons needed to form an image.
  • Electromagnetic Lenses: Made of magnetic coils that control and focus the electron beam. Function like glass lenses in a light microscope. Function: Focus and direct the electron beam onto the specimen.
  • Specimen Holder (Stage): Holds the sample in place inside the microscope. The specimen must be very thin (especially in TEM). Function: Positions the specimen for observation.
  • Vacuum System: Removes air inside the microscope. Prevents electrons from scattering. Function: Maintains a vacuum so electrons travel smoothly.
  • Image Viewing and Recording System: Converts electron signals into visible images. Can be a fluorescent screen, camera, or computer monitor. Function: Displays and records the magnified image.
  • Control System: Includes knobs, buttons, and software controls. Adjusts focus, brightness, and magnification. Function: Allows the user to operate and fine-tune the microscope.

Activity 3: From Colorful Cells to Invisible Structures

Directions: Read the passage about the light and electron microscope and answer the guide questions below.

“Cells are extremely small structures that cannot be seen clearly with the naked eye. Scientists use microscopes to observe cells and understand how living organism’s function. Two of the most important tools used in cell study are the Light Microscope and the Electron Microscope. Each microscope has unique advantages and limitations that help scientists study cells in different ways.

The light microscope uses visible light and a series of lenses to magnify objects. It is commonly used in classrooms and laboratories because it is easy to operate and relatively inexpensive. One of the major advantages of a light microscope is that it can be used to observe living cells. This allows scientists and students to see how cells move, grow, and divide in real time. Light microscopes can magnify objects up to about 1000 times, which is enough to observe the basic structures of a cell, such as the nucleus, cell membrane, and cytoplasm.

However, the light microscope also has limitations. Because it relies on light to magnify objects, its resolution is limited. Very tiny structures inside the cell, such as viruses or detailed parts of organelles, cannot be seen clearly using this microscope. As a result, scientists sometimes need a more powerful tool to observe smaller cellular components.

The electron microscope was developed to overcome these limitations. Instead of using light, it uses a beam of electrons to create an image. Because electrons have much shorter wavelengths than visible light, electron microscopes can produce images with extremely high magnification and resolution. This allows scientists to see very small structures inside cells, including organelles, membranes, and even viruses.

Despite its powerful magnification, the electron microscope also has some disadvantages. It is very expensive and requires specialized training to operate. In addition, specimens must be placed in a vacuum and prepared using special chemicals. Because of this process, living cells cannot be observed with an electron microscope. The images produced are usually black and white, although they may be colored later for easier interpretation.

Both microscopes play an important role in the study of cells. The light microscope is useful for observing living cells and studying their general structure and behavior. On the other hand, the electron microscope allows scientists to explore the detailed internal structures of cells that cannot be seen with light microscopes. By using both tools, scientists gain a deeper understanding of the complex world inside every cell.”
