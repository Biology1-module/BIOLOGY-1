Lesson 10: The Cell Membrane

This supplementary module is designed to help you understand the Cell Transport Mechanism. In this lesson, you will explore how the selective permeability of cell membrane protects and helps the cell to maintain homeostasis.

The cell membrane is a thin, flexible layer that surrounds the cell and separates it from its environment. It is made mainly of a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins that help regulate what enters and leaves the cell. This selective permeability allows the cell to maintain balance and proper functioning.

After going through this supplementary module, you are expected to:

  • Describe the structure and function of the cell membrane, including its selective permeability.
  • Explain how the cell membrane regulates the movement of substances in and out of the cell to maintain homeostasis.
  • Analyze the importance of the cell membrane in maintaining proper cellular conditions and supporting cell survival.

Structure of the Cell Membrane

The cell membrane is a flexible, dynamic structure that surrounds the cell and controls the movement of substances in and out. It follows the fluid mosaic model, meaning it is made of a phospholipid bilayer with proteins and other molecules embedded within it.

The main structure is the phospholipid bilayer, composed of hydrophilic (water-loving) heads and hydrophobic (water-fearing) tails. The hydrophilic heads face outward toward the watery environments inside and outside the cell, while the hydrophobic tails face inward, forming a barrier that prevents most substances from freely passing through. These phospholipids are a type of lipid, which gives the membrane its flexibility and structure.

Embedded within this bilayer are different types of proteins that perform specific functions:

  • Integral proteins span the entire membrane and are involved in transport and communication. Among them are channel proteins, which form pores that allow certain molecules or ions to pass through, and carrier proteins, which bind to substances and change shape to move them across the membrane.
  • Peripheral proteins are found on the surface of the membrane and often help with support, signaling, or maintaining the cell’s shape.

The membrane also contains glycoproteins and glycolipids, which have carbohydrate chains attached. These structures are important for cell recognition, communication, and adhesion, helping cells identify each other and interact properly. Another important component is cholesterol, which is found between the phospholipids. Cholesterol helps maintain the membrane’s fluidity and stability, preventing it from becoming too rigid in cold conditions or too fluid in warm conditions. Finally, the cytoskeleton is a network of protein fibers attached to the inner side of the membrane. It provides structural support, helps maintain cell shape, and assists in movement and organization within the cell.

Selective Permeability

The cell membrane is a selectively permeable barrier that regulates the movement of substances in and out of the cell to maintain homeostasis. It does not allow all substances to enter because uncontrolled entry could disrupt the cell’s internal balance, allow harmful materials inside, or cause the cell to swell or shrink.

Essential substances such as oxygen enter the cell for energy production, water moves in and out to maintain balance, glucose provides energy, and ions help regulate important processes like nerve signaling and muscle contraction, while carbon dioxide exits as a waste product. In contrast, large molecules like proteins and starch, as well as harmful substances such as toxins and pathogens, are restricted or blocked. By carefully selecting what enters and leaves, the cell membrane ensures that the cell remains stable, functional, and able to survive.
