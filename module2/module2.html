Lesson 2: The Cell

This supplementary material is designed to support and enhance students’ understanding of the core concepts in biology. These materials provide additional explanations, activities, examples, and assessments that help reinforce the lessons discussed in class or presented in the main learning module.

In the previous lesson, you learned about the origin of cells and the three postulates of the Cell Theory. In this lesson you will learn the structure and function of each organelle and how they carry out essential functions needed for growth, development, and survival. Cells contain small membrane-bound structures called organelles that performs specific functions.

After going through this supplementary module, you are expected to:

  • identify the different cell organelles;
  • describe the structure and functions of the cell organelles; and
  • relate the function of cell organelles in city-like illustration.

Nucleus

The nucleus is important to the life of the cell. It is commonly known as the “brain of the cell”. It contains and stores all the genetic information for manufacturing proteins and responsible for controlling all cellular activities.

  • The nucleus consists of chromatin, nucleolus, and nuclear envelope.
  • The chromatin is a network of strands that condenses and undergoes coiling into rod-like structures called chromosomes. Chromatin contains DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid), histones and some RNA (Ribonucleic Acid).
  • The nucleolus is a dark region of chromatin where a type of RNA, called ribosomal RNA (rRNA), is produced, and joins with proteins to form the subunits of ribosomes.
  • The nuclear envelope is a double membrane that separates the nucleus from the cytoplasm. It has nuclear pores to permit the passage of RNA out of the nucleus into the cytoplasm, and the passage of proteins from then cytoplasm into the nucleus.
  • The nucleus serves as the storage of genetic materials such as DNA and RNA.
  • Nucleus controls other organelles by facilitating cell growth, cell division, and protein synthesis.

Ribosomes

The ribosomes are also known as the “protein factories of the cell”.

  • They are mainly responsible for protein synthesis in the cells.
  • They are usually dispersed in the cytoplasm or attached to the endoplasmic reticulum.
  • Ribosomes are composed of one large subunit and one small subunit.
  • The number of ribosomes in a cell varies depending on its functions. For example, pancreatic cells have many ribosomes because they produce secretions that contain proteins.
  • Ribosomes receive mRNA from the nucleus, and this nucleic acid carries a coded message from DNA. The mRNA carries the correct sequence of amino acids in a protein. Protein synthesized by the cytoplasmic ribosomes are used in the cytoplasm, while proteins synthesized by attached ribosomes end up in the endoplasmic reticulum.

The Endomembrane System

The endomembrane system consists of the three major organelles that work together to perform different tasks such as packaging, producing, and exporting certain cellular products. The endomembrane system includes the endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus and several types of vesicles.

  • The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) consists of complicated system of membranous channels and flattened vesicles which is physically continuous to the outer membrane of the nuclear envelope.
  • There are two types of endoplasmic reticulum: the rough endoplasmic reticulum and smooth endoplasmic reticulum.
  • Rough endoplasmic reticulum is studded with ribosomes on the side of the membrane that faces the cytoplasm. It is responsible for synthesizing proteins. Rough endoplasmic reticulum forms vesicles in which large molecules are transported to other parts of the cell.
  • Smooth endoplasmic reticulum which is continuous with rough ER, does not have attached ribosomes. Smooth endoplasmic reticulum is responsible for synthesizing lipids, such as phospholipids and steroids. The function of smooth ER is usually dependent on a particular cell. For example, smooth ER in the liver helps in detoxifying drugs. Usually, smooth ER increases its quantity when a person regularly consumes alcohols.

Golgi Apparatus (Dictyosomes)

  • The Golgi apparatus are a stack of curved, flattened sacs, which can be compared to a pile of pancakes.
  • Mostly, one side of the stack (the cis) is directed towards the endoplasmic reticulum, and the other side of the stack (the trans) is directed towards the cell membrane.
  • The Golgi apparatus receives vesicles from the endoplasmic reticulum along the cis face. Once inside the Golgi apparatus, the material is altered in the lumen of the vesicle. The vesicles then start to swell and be released from the trans face. These vesicles may be either exported outside the cell or may be used by the cell for its own use.

Lysosomes

  • The lysosomes are membrane-bounded vesicles produced by the Golgi apparatus.
  • Lysosomes are very acidic and contain powerful hydrolytic digestive enzymes that require a low–pH environment. Hydrolytic enzymes break down large molecules into small molecules. For example, they break down large carbohydrates into simple sugars that will serve as a nutrient for the cell.
  • Lysosomes are responsible in recycling cellular material and destroying invading viruses and bacteria.
  • Lysosomes also participate in a programmed cell death called apoptosis which is a normal part of development. For example, the fingers of human embryo are at webbed at first, but eventually freed from one another because of lysosomal action.

Energy–Related Organelles

One of the functions of the cell is to convert energy to a form that can be used by the cell. It includes organelles such as mitochondria and chloroplasts.

Mitochondrion

The mitochondrion (mitochondria, plural) is often called the “powerhouse of the cell.” It produces most of the ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) which is used by the cell through the process called cellular respiration.

  • The mitochondrion is a double membrane-bound organelle that is responsible for supplying and storing energy for the cell. It has two membranes, the outer membrane and the inner membrane. The inner membrane is highly convoluted into cristae that project into the matrix. The inner membrane surrounds a matrix containing its own mitochondrial DNA and ribosomes. The matrix is a highly concentrated mixture of enzymes that break down carbohydrates and other nutrient molecules.
  • The mitochondrion is where the synthesis of energy in the form of ATP takes place which is required for other cell organelles to function.
  • Mitochondria also help in the balancing of cations within the cell and assists the process of apoptosis. Apoptosis is also known as “cellular suicide”. It is a convenient way of removing cells that should not be longer part of the cell.
  • Mitochondria in the liver can detoxify ammonia.

Chloroplasts

  • Chloroplasts are plant and algal organelles that are bounded by a double membrane.
  • A chloroplast contains a series of internal membrane or vesicles. It contains DNA and chlorophyll which carry on photosynthesis. Its double membrane encloses a large space called stroma.

Peroxisomes

  • The peroxisomes are membrane-bounded vesicles that encloses enzymes.
  • The enzymes in the peroxisomes are synthesized by free ribosomes and transported into the cytoplasm.
  • Each peroxisome also carries enzymes which results in the formation of hydrogen peroxide (H2O2).
  • Peroxisomes can also detoxify some products of photosynthesis.

Answer: If the mitochondria stopped working, the cell would not be able to produce enough energy (ATP) to carry out its functions. As a result, the cell would weaken and eventually die. Answer: If the government stopped functioning properly, the city would become disorganized, with no rules or coordination, leading to chaos. Similarly, if the nucleus stopped working, the cell would lose control of its activities since it contains the genetic instructions. This would result in improper functioning and could cause the cell to die.
Answer: It is important that all organelles work together because each one has a specific role that supports the cell’s survival. If one organelle fails, it can affect the entire cell. Just like in a city, where different systems (power, transportation, communication) must work together, the cell relies on coordination among organelles to function properly and stay alive.
