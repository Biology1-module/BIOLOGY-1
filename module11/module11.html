Lesson 11: Cellular Abnormalities

This supplementary module is designed to help you understand Cellular Abnormalities. In this lesson, you will explore how cellular abnormalities lead to diseases such as cancer, diabetes, and genetic disorders.

Cellular abnormalities are changes in a cell’s structure, function, or genetic material that disrupt its normal behavior. These may result from mutations, environmental factors, or errors in cell division, leading to malfunctioning cells. Such abnormalities can contribute to diseases, including cancer, when cells grow uncontrollably or fail to function properly.

After going through this supplementary module, you are expected to:

  • Identify and interpret information from secondary sources about cellular abnormalities related to diseases.
  • Explain how specific cellular abnormalities such as uncontrolled cell division, malfunctioning cells, genetic mutations lead to diseases such as cancer, diabetes, and genetic disorders.
  • Analyze the impact of these cellular abnormalities on body systems and evaluate their role in disease development and progression.

Types of Cellular Abnormalities

Cellular abnormalities occur when normal cell processes are disrupted, and these changes can lead to various diseases. Three major types of abnormalities include uncontrolled cell division, malfunctioning cells, and genetic mutations, each affecting the body in different ways.

  • Uncontrolled cell division happens when cells divide continuously without proper regulation. Normally, the cell cycle is tightly controlled, but when this control fails—often due to damaged genes—cells keep multiplying. This leads to the formation of tumors and is the main cause of cancer, where abnormal cells can invade nearby tissues and spread throughout the body.
  • Malfunctioning cells occur when cells are present but do not perform their normal functions properly. For example, in diabetes, cells either do not produce enough insulin or do not respond to it correctly, preventing glucose from entering the cells. As a result, blood sugar levels rise, affecting the body’s ability to maintain balance and leading to long-term health problems.
  • Genetic mutations are changes in the DNA sequence that can alter how proteins are made or function. These mutations can be inherited or acquired over time. When important proteins are affected, normal cellular processes are disrupted. This can lead to genetic disorders such as cystic fibrosis, where a defective protein causes thick mucus to build up in the lungs and other organs.

Source 1: Cancer (Cell Division Problem)

Cancer occurs when cells divide uncontrollably due to mutations in genes that regulate the cell cycle. These abnormal cells form tumors and can spread to other parts of the body (metastasis). Normally, cell division is controlled, but cancer cells ignore these controls.

Source 2: Diabetes (Cell Signaling Problem)

Diabetes is a disease in which the body cannot properly regulate blood sugar levels. In Type 1 diabetes, the immune system destroys insulin-producing cells. In Type 2 diabetes, cells do not respond properly to insulin. This leads to high glucose levels in the blood.

Source 3: Genetic Disorder (Mutation Problem)

Genetic disorders are caused by mutations in DNA. For example, cystic fibrosis results from a defective protein that controls salt and water movement in cells. This causes thick mucus to build up in organs like the lungs.
