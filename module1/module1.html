What I Need to Know

Lesson 1: The Cell Theory

This supplementary module is designed to help you understand the historical development of Cell Theory, one of the most important foundations in biology. In this lesson, you will explore how scientific knowledge develops through the observations and discoveries of different scientists over time.

Cell theory is one of the fundamental principles in biology; it explains the basic structure and function of all living organisms. Cell theory lies in its role as the foundation for studying life. Understanding cell theory helps explain how organisms grow, develop, and carry out the processes necessary for survival.

After going through this supplementary module, you are expected to:

  • Analyze the development of cell theory thru historical analysis of scientific information;
  • Organize historical timeline of discoveries of cell theory; and
  • Explain how individual discoveries contributed to cell theory.

The Three Main Principles of Cell Theory

  1. All living organisms are made up of one or more cells.
    Every living thing, from single-celled bacteria to complex organisms like plants and animals, is composed of cells.
  2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and function in living organisms.
    Cells carry out all the processes necessary for life, such as metabolism, growth, and reproduction.
  3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells.
    New cells are formed through the division of existing cells.

Activity 2: Secondary Source Investigation

Directions: Read the passage about cell theory and complete the historical analysis table below by dragging the correct phrases from the pool into the empty boxes.

📱 Playing on a phone? Tap an item in the box to select it, then tap an empty box in the table to place it!

"The cell theory is one of the most important ideas in biology because it explains that cells are the basic units of life. This theory was developed through the discoveries and observations of several scientists who studied microscopic structures using early microscopes.

In 1665, Robert Hooke observed a thin slice of cork using a compound microscope. He noticed many tiny box-like compartments and called them cells, because they reminded him of small rooms. Although Hooke observed dead cells from plant tissue, his work introduced the concept that living things are made up of small structural units.

Several years later, in 1674 Antonie van Leeuwenhoek improved the microscope and became the first person to observe living microorganisms in pond water and other samples. He called these tiny living organisms “animalcules.” His discoveries showed that microscopic life exists and that cells can be living organisms.

In 1838, Matthias Schleiden studied plant tissues and concluded that all plants are composed of cells. His research helped scientists understand that cells are the basic units that make up plant structures.

Later, in 1855, Rudolf Virchow proposed that all cells come from pre-existing cells. This idea explained how cells reproduce and how organisms grow and develop.

Together, the discoveries of these scientists helped establish the cell theory, which states that all living organisms are made of cells, the cell is the basic unit of life, and new cells arise from existing cells."
