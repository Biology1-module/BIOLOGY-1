What I Need to Know

Lesson 9: Cell Transport Mechanisms

This supplementary module is designed to help you understand the Cell Transport Mechanism. In this lesson, you will explore the difference between passive and active transport, their energy use, direction of movement, and mechanisms involved.

Cells maintain balance by controlling how substances move in and out through the cell membrane. This occurs through passive transport (diffusion, osmosis, and facilitated diffusion), which does not require energy, and active transport (pumps, endocytosis, and exocytosis), which uses energy to move materials. These transport mechanisms are essential for maintaining homeostasis and supporting cell survival.

After going through this supplementary module, you are expected to:

  • Identify and describe the different types of passive transport (diffusion, osmosis, and facilitated diffusion) and explain how substances move across the cell membrane without energy.
  • Explain the processes of active transport (membrane pumps, endocytosis, and exocytosis), including how energy is used to move substances against the concentration gradient.
  • Compare and differentiate passive and active transport in terms of energy use, direction of movement, and mechanisms involved.

Passive Transport

Passive transport is the movement of substances across the cell membrane without the use of energy (ATP). In all forms of passive transport, particles move from an area of higher concentration to lower concentration, helping the cell maintain balance or homeostasis. There are three main types:

  • Diffusion: The simplest form of passive transport, where small molecules like oxygen and carbon dioxide move directly across the cell membrane. These molecules spread out until they are evenly distributed, reaching a state of equilibrium.
  • Osmosis: The diffusion of water molecules across a selectively permeable membrane. Water moves from an area of higher water concentration (lower solute concentration) to an area of lower water concentration (higher solute concentration). This process is important for maintaining the proper water balance in cells. For example, plant cells can swell or shrink depending on the surrounding solution.
  • Facilitated Diffusion: Involves the movement of larger or charged molecules, such as glucose and ions, across the membrane with the help of protein channels or carrier proteins. Although it requires assistance from these proteins, it still does not use energy and follows the concentration gradient.

Overall, passive transport allows essential substances to enter and exit the cell efficiently without energy, ensuring that cells function properly and remain in a stable internal environment.
