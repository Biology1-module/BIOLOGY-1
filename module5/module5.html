Lesson 5: Phase of Cell Cycle and Their Control Points

This supplementary module is designed to help you understand the phases of cell cycle and their control points. In this lesson, you will explore how the importance of cell cycle control points and identify key events in each phase.

The cell cycle is a highly organized series of events that cells go through as they grow, replicate their DNA, and divide into two new cells. To ensure accurate cell division, the cell cycle is regulated by specific control points (checkpoints). These control mechanisms help prevent errors, maintain genetic stability, and protect the organism from conditions such as uncontrolled cell growth.

After going through this supplementary module, you are expected to:

  • Describe the phases of the cell cycle (interphase, M-phase, and cytokinesis) and identify their key events.
  • Sequence the stages of the cell cycle and analyze the role of each checkpoint (G1, G2, and M) in regulating cell division.
  • Explain the importance of cell cycle control points by predicting the consequences of checkpoint failure on cell function.

The Cell Cycle Phases

The cell cycle is a continuous process by which a cell grows, replicates its DNA, and divides to form two identical daughter cells. It ensures proper growth, repair, and reproduction in living organisms. The cycle is divided into two major parts: Interphase (G₁, S, G₂) and the M Phase (mitosis and cytokinesis).

Interphase

The interphase of the cell cycle consists of the G₁, S, and G₂ phases, during which the cell grows, replicates its DNA, and prepares for division.

  • G₁ Phase: The cell increases in size, produces organelles such as mitochondria and ribosomes, and carries out normal metabolic functions like protein synthesis. It also accumulates energy and synthesizes RNA and proteins needed for DNA replication. During this stage, the cell checks whether conditions are favorable for division and may enter a resting stage (G₀) if conditions are not suitable.
  • S Phase: DNA replication occurs, where the genetic material is copied to produce identical sister chromatids. Each chromosome duplicates, forming two identical strands joined at the centromere. Histone proteins are produced to help package the newly replicated DNA, and centrosomes begin to duplicate. Although the DNA content doubles, the number of chromosomes remains the same. The cell also uses proofreading mechanisms to ensure that errors during DNA replication are minimized.
  • G₂ Phase: The cell continues to grow and prepares for mitosis. It synthesizes proteins necessary for cell division, including those involved in forming spindle fibers. Organelles and other cellular components are further produced, and microtubules begin to form. Importantly, the cell checks the replicated DNA for any errors and repairs them to ensure accurate division. This phase serves as the final checkpoint before the cell enters mitosis.

M Phase (Mitosis and Cytokinesis)

During the M phase of the cell cycle, the cell undergoes mitosis followed by cytokinesis, resulting in two identical daughter cells.

  • Prophase: The chromosomes condense and become visible under a microscope, with each chromosome consisting of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere. The nuclear membrane begins to break down, the nucleolus disappears, and centrosomes move toward opposite poles of the cell. At the same time, spindle fibers start to form and begin attaching to the chromosomes.
  • Metaphase: The chromosomes align along the center of the cell, known as the metaphase plate. Spindle fibers are fully attached to the centromeres (kinetochores) of each chromosome, ensuring that each sister chromatid is connected to opposite poles. The cell checks that all chromosomes are properly aligned before proceeding.
  • Anaphase: The sister chromatids separate at the centromere and are pulled toward opposite poles of the cell by the spindle fibers. As this occurs, the cell elongates, and each chromatid is now considered an individual chromosome.
  • Telophase: The chromosomes reach the opposite poles and begin to decondense back into chromatin. Nuclear membranes reform around each set of chromosomes, nucleoli reappear, and the spindle fibers start to break down.
  • Cytokinesis: The cytoplasm divides to form two separate daughter cells. In animal cells, a cleavage furrow forms and pinches the cell membrane, while in plant cells, a cell plate forms and develops into a new cell wall. Each daughter cell contains identical genetic material and enters the G₁ phase.

Cell Cycle Checkpoints

The cell cycle checkpoints are control mechanisms that ensure a cell only proceeds to the next stage when conditions are correct and safe. These checkpoints act like “quality control points” during the cell cycle. Each checkpoint monitors key events in the cell and prevents errors that could lead to damaged or abnormal cells.

  • G₁ Checkpoint: The cell evaluates whether it is ready to begin DNA replication. During this stage, the cell must have enough nutrients, sufficient size, and undamaged DNA. If DNA damage is detected or conditions are unfavorable, the checkpoint stops the cycle to allow repair or may direct the cell to a resting stage. If this checkpoint fails, damaged DNA may be copied during the S phase, which can lead to mutations or diseases such as cancer.
  • G₂ Checkpoint: Occurs after DNA replication in the S phase. At this point, the cell ensures that DNA has been completely and accurately copied into identical sister chromatids. It also checks for any replication errors and prepares for mitosis. If DNA is incomplete or damaged, the checkpoint halts progression to allow repairs. Failure of the G₂ checkpoint may result in cells entering mitosis with errors, leading to improper chromosome separation and defective daughter cells.
  • M Checkpoint (Spindle Checkpoint): Takes place during metaphase of mitosis. Here, the cell verifies that all chromosomes are properly aligned at the center (metaphase plate) and that spindle fibers are correctly attached to each chromosome. If chromosomes are not properly attached or aligned, the checkpoint prevents the cell from proceeding. If this checkpoint fails, chromosomes may not separate equally, resulting in daughter cells with abnormal chromosome numbers (aneuploidy).

Overall, these checkpoints are essential for maintaining the accuracy of cell division. When checkpoints fail, it can lead to serious consequences such as genetic mutations, uncontrolled cell division, or cell death.
